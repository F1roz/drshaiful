Minimal Access (Laparoscopic) Surgery

Home › Expertise › Minimal Access (Laparoscopic) Surgery

Overview

Minimal access, or laparoscopic, surgery uses small incisions, a camera, and fine instruments in place of a large traditional opening. In children this approach is used for conditions ranging from appendicitis and gallbladder disease to complex intra-abdominal procedures, offering less postoperative pain, smaller scars, and typically a faster return to normal activity.

The Procedure

Carbon dioxide gas is used to create working space within the abdomen, and the operation is completed through a small number of 3–12mm incisions using a camera and specialised instruments. Depending on the underlying condition, conversion to an open approach may occasionally be needed for safety.

Preparing for Surgery

Fasting and pre-admission instructions are provided according to the specific procedure being performed.

Recovery

Recovery is generally faster than with open surgery, though it varies by procedure; specific guidance is given for each operation.

Possible Complications

General surgical risks apply, including a small chance of conversion to open surgery, along with the specific risks of the underlying operation being performed.

Follow-up

Follow-up timing depends on the specific procedure performed.
